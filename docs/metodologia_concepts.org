* Introduction
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:introduccion
:END:

triage-pg is a PostgreSQL-native framework for temporal machine learning on
tabular public-policy data. Anyone can wire together a cohort query, some
features, and a classifier; what is hard --- and what this document is about
--- is making the resulting numbers *trustworthy* and *reproducible* enough to
put in front of a partner agency and act on.

Four design decisions carry that weight. They are documented tersely in the
Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) as *what we decided*, and pedagogically on
the docs site's Concepts pages as *how it works*. This companion is the third
register: the *long why* --- the reasoning, the alternatives we rejected, and
the failure modes each decision is defending against --- written for a mixed
audience of partners, coworkers, and students who need to understand the
approach, not just run it.

The four foundations are:

1. *Point-in-time correctness* and the imputation leakage boundary --- what
   makes an offline metric honest.
2. *Artifact identity and caching* --- a Guix-style derivation DAG that makes
   every result reproducible and cacheable.
3. *The ranking spine* --- treating the whole system as one
   score → rank → evaluate pipeline that a ~problem_type~ discriminator
   specializes.
4. *The data model* --- one database per project plus a registry control
   plane, append-only predictions, and matrices on object storage.

The narrated, exhaustive reference for each lives on the docs site under
/Concepts/; the terse decision boundary lives in the ADRs. This document
explains why each was chosen over the alternatives.

* Motivation --- why trustworthiness must be engineered
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:motivacion
:END:

A prioritization model that scores citizens, cases, or facilities is only
useful if two properties hold that ordinary ML tooling does *not* give you for
free:

- *The offline estimate must predict the online reality.* The single most
  common way this fails is *leakage*: the model is inadvertently shown, at
  training time, information it could not possibly have at prediction time.
  Leakage inflates cross-validation metrics and then collapses in production ---
  the most expensive failure mode in applied ML because it is invisible until
  deployment.
- *A result must be reproducible and auditable.* Public-policy decisions are
  contestable. "Model 42 said so" is not defensible unless one can answer, in
  SQL, "exactly which inputs, code, and data versions produced Model 42, and
  can we rebuild it bit-for-bit?"

These are not features you bolt on afterward; they are properties of the
substrate. The four foundations below each exist to guarantee one of them by
construction, so that a correct pipeline is the path of least resistance and an
incorrect one is hard to express.

* Foundation 1 --- Point-in-time correctness and the imputation boundary
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:point-in-time
:END:

** The cardinal rule

Every training and scoring example is a pair \((\text{entity}, \text{as\_of\_date})\).
The *cardinal rule* is:

#+begin_quote
A feature computed for an ~as_of_date~ may use only data that was knowable
*strictly before* that date.
#+end_quote

The subtlety is that "knowable before" is not "occurred before". A lab result
dated last Tuesday may not have been *entered into the system* until this
Friday. If a feature keys off the event date, it silently leaks the future. So
triage-pg distinguishes the *knowledge date* --- when a fact became available
--- from the *event date*, and every feature declares a ~knowledge_date_column~
that must reflect the former. This is the difference between an honest backtest
and a fantasy.

** Why not a random train/test split?

The default reflex from general ML --- shuffle the rows, hold out 20% --- is
actively wrong here. A random split lets the model train on August to predict
July; it learns from the future. Temporal problems require *temporal*
validation: train on the past, test on a strictly later window. triage-pg's
temporal splitting (inherited from ~timechop~) generates ~as_of_dates~ so that
every split respects the arrow of time. The random split is not a weaker
option; it is a different, wrong experiment.

** The imputation leakage boundary

Missing values are where leakage sneaks back in even after the split is
correct. Imputing a missing feature with "the mean" is fitting a statistic ---
and *if that mean is computed over the whole matrix, it has seen the test
rows*. triage-pg therefore splits imputation into two regimes with a hard
boundary between them (ADR-0009):

#+latex: \rowcolors[]{1}{}{gray!10}
#+attr_latex: :placement [H]
#+attr_latex: :environment tabular :font \small :center t :booktabs t :rmlines t :align L{0.16\textwidth}|L{0.40\textwidth}|L{0.34\textwidth}
#+caption: The two imputation regimes and the leakage boundary between them.
#+name: tbl:imputation
| Regime         | What it does                                                        | Where it runs                                    |
|----------------+---------------------------------------------------------------------+--------------------------------------------------|
| *Fit-free*     | Fill with a constant (0 for counts, a sentinel) + a ~*_imp~ missing-flag column | In the feature engine (featurizer); needs no data |
| *Fit-based*    | Fill with a learned statistic (mean / median / mode)                | In the triage-pg adapter, *fitted on the training split only* |

The rule that falls out is a single sentence a student can memorize: *never fit
an imputation statistic over the full matrix.* The fit-based statistics are
computed on the train split, persisted, and then *applied* --- never re-fitted
--- to the test split. In the derivation DAG (Foundation 2) this shows up as an
explicit edge: the test matrix takes the train matrix as a parent because it
consumes the train-fitted statistics. The leakage boundary is not a coding
convention one might forget; it is a structural property of the artifact graph.

* Foundation 2 --- Artifact identity and caching
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:identity
:END:

** The requirement

Every built artifact --- cohort, labels, feature group, matrix, model,
predictions, evaluations --- must be able to answer, in SQL, "what exact inputs
produced you?" and must be rebuilt bit-for-bit from those inputs. This is the
reproducibility half of the motivation, and it is borrowed wholesale from
functional package management: the Guix store (Courtès, 2013) gives a package a
hash over its *complete build closure*, and four properties fall out for free.

triage-pg applies the same idea to ML artifacts (ADR-0013 through ADR-0017). An
artifact's identity --- its *Derivation* --- is a SHA-256 over four things,
serialized as canonical JSON:

#+begin_export latex
\begin{align}
\text{artifact\_id} = H\big(\; & \text{kind} \;\Vert\; \text{canonical}(\text{own\_config}) \nonumber \\
   \;\Vert\; & \text{sorted}(\text{parent\_ids}) \;\Vert\; \text{sorted}(\text{source\_pins}) \;\Vert\; \text{sorted}(\text{engine\_versions}) \;\big) \label{eq:derivation}
\end{align}
#+end_export

Because each parent's id is embedded, this is a *Merkle DAG*: a matrix's id
embeds its cohort/label/feature ids, a model's id embeds its matrix id. Any
upstream change ripples downward automatically. Four properties follow, exactly
as in the Guix store:

1. *Exact cache reuse* --- identical inputs yield an identical hash, so the
   build is skipped.
2. *Minimal incremental rebuilds* --- a changed input invalidates precisely its
   downstream cone, nothing more.
3. *Provenance* --- any artifact can report its full input closure in SQL.
4. *Garbage collection by reachability* --- outputs unreachable from a retained
   experiment root can be deleted (matters most for Parquet matrices on disk).

** Why not hash the config text?

The inherited DSSG triage hashes configuration *text*. That is fragile in both
directions: a semantically identical config with reordered keys hashes
differently (spurious cache misses), and --- worse --- two runs with the same
config text but a *different code version or a different upstream data snapshot*
hash identically (silent, dangerous cache *hits*). Hashing the full closure
(equation \eqref{eq:derivation}) fixes both: ~source_pins~ (ADR-0014) fold the
exact data snapshot into identity, and ~engine_versions~ (ADR-0016) fold the
code into it.

The estimator's own library version is part of *model* identity, because a
model trained by ~scikit-learn~ 1.4 is not the same artifact as one trained by
1.8. This is not academic: resolving that version robustly across installation
modes was a real bug --- on an editable install the import-package-to-
distribution map can be absent --- and the fix had to preserve identity exactly
so that cache keys and published result hashes did not move.

* Foundation 3 --- The ranking spine
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:ranking
:END:

triage-pg is, above all, a *prioritization* system: its job is to order
entities so a limited intervention budget goes to the right ones. Its entire
architecture is therefore one spine --- *produce a score → rank entities →
evaluate the ranking* --- and the ~problem_type~ on an Experiment selects a few
swaps on that spine (ADR-0010).

#+latex: \rowcolors[]{1}{}{gray!10}
#+attr_latex: :placement [H]
#+attr_latex: :environment tabular :font \small :center t :booktabs t :rmlines t :align L{0.20\textwidth}|L{0.18\textwidth}|L{0.22\textwidth}|L{0.22\textwidth}
#+caption: The four problem types as swaps on the score → rank → evaluate spine.
#+name: tbl:problem-types
| ~problem_type~       | score is…            | rank by…             | label columns              |
|----------------------+----------------------+----------------------+----------------------------|
| ~classification~     | \(P(y=1)\)           | descending probability | ~outcome~ (0/1)          |
| ~regression_ranking~ | predicted value      | descending value       | ~outcome~ (continuous)   |
| ~regression~         | predicted value      | descending value       | ~outcome~ (continuous)   |
| ~survival~           | risk score           | descending risk        | ~duration, event_observed~ |

Two consequences a practitioner must internalize. First, *the label columns
follow the ~problem_type~* --- a hard contract the label query must satisfy
(~outcome~ for most; the ~(duration, event_observed)~ pair for the survival
path, ADR-0026). Second, ~problem_type~ is part of the Experiment's *identity*
(ADR-0022): changing it is a *different problem*, not a new attempt at the same
one.

Orthogonal to all of this is ~task_framing~ (~early_warning~ /
~resource_prioritization~ / ~visit_level~), which is identity-neutral metadata:
it changes how one should *read* the numbers --- who gets a label and why ---
not how they are computed.

** Why one spine instead of per-problem pipelines?

The alternative is a separate pipeline per problem type: a classification
pipeline, a regression pipeline, a survival pipeline. That triples the surface
area, triples the places a leakage or evaluation bug can hide, and makes
cross-problem comparison incoherent. Collapsing everything to score → rank →
evaluate means the temporal machinery, the imputation boundary, the identity
DAG, and the evaluation code are written *once* and shared; only the three
narrow swap points differ. The cost --- occasionally contorting a problem to
fit the spine --- is far smaller than maintaining parallel stacks.

* Foundation 4 --- The data model
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:data-model
:END:

** One database per project, plus a registry

Each *Project* is one isolated PostgreSQL *database* in a shared cluster, and a
dedicated *registry* database is the control plane holding projects, users,
routing and connection info, permissions, and web-app auth (ADR-0002). Teardown
is a single ~DROP DATABASE~; there is no risk of one project's rows bleeding
into another's queries because cross-project SQL is, by construction, not
native.

The rejected alternative --- a single shared database with a ~project_id~
column on every table (multi-tenancy by discriminator) --- is cheaper to set up
and far more dangerous: every query must remember the ~WHERE project_id = …~
filter, and the one that forgets silently mixes tenants. Isolation at the
database boundary makes that class of bug impossible rather than merely
discouraged.

** What lives where

Only *predictions* and *evaluations* live in Postgres. Matrices are Parquet and
serialized models are binaries, both on the local filesystem or S3; the
database holds only thin pointer rows. This keeps the database small and fast
(metrics need only ~(entity_id, score, label)~, which is in Postgres regardless
of where the matrices live) and lets large artifacts live on cheap object
storage.

#+latex: \rowcolors[]{1}{}{gray!10}
#+attr_latex: :placement [H]
#+attr_latex: :environment tabular :font \small :center t :booktabs t :rmlines t :align L{0.34\textwidth}|L{0.50\textwidth}
#+caption: Where each kind of state lives.
#+name: tbl:where
| State                                     | Home                              |
|-------------------------------------------+-----------------------------------|
| predictions, evaluations, lineage         | the per-project ~triage~ schema (PostgreSQL) |
| matrices, serialized models               | Parquet / binaries on local FS or S3 |
| projects, users, routing, permissions     | the registry database             |

** Append-only predictions: a score is not the latest score

Every scoring run *inserts* prediction rows stamped with a ~scored_at~
timestamp; nothing is ever overwritten (ADR-0006). The table is time-
partitioned and kept forever by default. The consequence a reader must
internalize is the counter-intuitive slogan: *a score is not the latest score.*
A query that wants the "current" prediction for an entity must explicitly pick
the row with the maximum ~scored_at~; the naive "select the score" returns a
history, not a value.

The rejected alternative --- upserting, so each entity has exactly one "current"
score --- throws away the monitoring use case (drift, volume, calibration over
time all need the history) and destroys auditability (one cannot reconstruct
what the model said last quarter). Append-only is cheap insurance: storage is
cheap, and the history is the raw material for monitoring.

* Design choices at a glance
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:design-choices
:END:

#+latex: \rowcolors[]{1}{}{gray!10}
#+attr_latex: :placement [H]
#+attr_latex: :environment tabular :font \small :center t :booktabs t :rmlines t :align L{0.30\textwidth}|L{0.26\textwidth}|L{0.30\textwidth}
#+caption: The four foundations as chosen-over-rejected decisions.
#+name: tbl:choices
| Decision                          | Chosen                          | Rejected (and why)                                   |
|-----------------------------------+---------------------------------+------------------------------------------------------|
| Validation split                  | temporal (train past → test future) | random split (learns from the future)            |
| Imputation                        | fit-free + train-only fit-based | fit over the full matrix (leaks the test set)        |
| Artifact identity                 | hash of the full input closure  | hash of config text (spurious misses, silent hits)   |
| System shape                      | one score → rank → evaluate spine | per-problem pipelines (3× surface, incoherent comparison) |
| Multi-project isolation           | one database per project        | shared DB + ~project_id~ filter (silent tenant mixing) |
| Prediction storage                | append-only with ~scored_at~    | upsert (loses history + monitoring + audit)          |

* Data and provenance
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:datos
:END:

The concepts above are not abstractions; they are enforced by specific code and
schema. For readers who will maintain the system:

- *Temporal splits* --- ~src/triage/component/timechop/~ turns a
  ~temporal_config~ into the ~as_of_dates~ every split is built for.
- *Imputation boundary* --- fit-free logic lives in the ~featurizer~ dependency;
  fit-based logic in ~src/triage/adapters/{imputation,matrix}.py~, fitted on the
  train split and persisted with the matrix.
- *Derivation identity* --- ~src/triage/derivation.py~ (~engine_versions_for~,
  the hashing) and ~src/triage/sources.py~ (source pinning); the schema lives in
  ~artifacts~, ~artifact_inputs~, ~sources~, ~source_versions~.
- *The ranking spine* --- ~src/triage/component/catwalk/~ (estimators, in-PG
  evaluation) reads ~triage.prediction_ranks ⋈ triage.labels~.
- *Append-only predictions* --- the ~predictions~ table, range-partitioned on
  ~scored_at~; forward scoring is ~src/triage/adapters/forward.py~.
- *Project isolation* --- ~src/triage/registry.py~ and
  ~src/triage/project_lifecycle.py~ over the registry database.

The full entity-relationship diagram is in [[file:erd.md][the ERD]]; the derivation DAG
design is in [[file:derivation-dag.md][the derivation-DAG document]]; the schema rationale is in
[[file:schema-design.md][the schema-design document]].

* Assumptions and limitations
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec:supuestos
:END:

- *Tabular, temporal data.* The whole apparatus assumes entities with dated
  events. It is not a framework for images, free text, or non-temporal problems.
- *Point-in-time correctness is only as good as the ~knowledge_date_column~.*
  If a data provider back-dates records, or the declared knowledge date is
  wrong, the framework cannot detect the resulting leakage --- it trusts the
  declaration.
- *Content-addressing assumes deterministic builds.* Non-deterministic feature
  code or estimators (unseeded randomness) would produce identical hashes for
  different outputs, breaking the cache-reuse guarantee. Seeds are recorded on
  the run for this reason.
- *Append-only storage grows without bound.* The default is keep-forever;
  operators who score at high frequency must plan partition retention.
- *One database per project* trades cross-project SQL for isolation. Genuinely
  cross-project analysis must be done at the application layer, not in SQL.
